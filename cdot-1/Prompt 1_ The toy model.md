Prompt 1: *In a toy-model of the universe, where the redshift is produced by the speed of light being proportionally lower at a given redshift, and no motion or expansion is presumed, what would be the distance to a galaxy at z=1?*

In this toy model, the distance to the galaxy **cannot be determined** because the model provides no mathematical relationship linking redshift (or the speed of light) to physical distance. \[1, 2\]

Here is the breakdown of why the distance remains completely underdetermined:

## **1\. What the Model Tells Us**

* **Constant Frequency:** Without cosmic expansion or physical motion, a photon's frequency ($\\nu$) remains constant during its entire journey.  
* **Wavelength and Speed:** The wavelength of light is governed by the formula $\\lambda \= c / \\nu$.  
* **Speed at Emission:** To produce a redshift ($1 \+ z \= \\lambda\_{\\text{obs}} / \\lambda\_{\\text{emit}}$), the speed of light at the source must be lower: $c\_{\\text{emit}} \= c\_{\\text{obs}} / (1 \+ z)$.  
* **Value at z=1:** For a galaxy at $z \= 1$, the speed of light at the moment of emission would be exactly **half of its current value** ($0.5c$). \[3, 4\]

## **2\. Why Distance is Missing**

* **No Spatial Rule:** The model states that light is slower at a given redshift, but it does not specify *where* or *when* this happens.  
* **Arbitrary Placement:** A galaxy could be 10 light-years away or 10 billion light-years away.  
* **Missing Parameter:** As long as the local speed of light at that galaxy's location is $0.5c$, it will always show a redshift of $z \= 1$, regardless of its distance from Earth. \[5, 6\]

To calculate an actual distance, the toy model would need an additional rule. For example, a formula specifying how the speed of light drops per megaparsec of distance traveled.
